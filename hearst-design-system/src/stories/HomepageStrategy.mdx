# Homepage Layout Strategy

Three layout systems designed to maximize conversion, return visits, and time-on-site across 29 Hearst brands. Each layout targets a different reader behavior profile based on research from high-performing magazine and digital media sites.

## The Three Layouts

<LayoutCard name="A: The Curator" subtitle="NYT-inspired editorial hierarchy" brands="Esquire, Cosmopolitan, Elle, Harper's Bazaar, Town & Country">

Targets the **lean-back reader** who trusts editorial judgment. A strong 60/40 hero split with numbered top stories creates a clear reading path. The editorial voice is front and center, guiding users through curated sections rather than asking them to browse.

**Core principle:** Reduce decision fatigue. The fewer choices a reader has to make, the deeper they go.

</LayoutCard>

<LayoutCard name="B: The Mosaic" subtitle="Verge/TIME-inspired modular grid" brands="Elle, Harper's Bazaar, House Beautiful, Veranda, Delish">

Targets the **visual browser** who scans by image before reading headlines. A bento-grid hero surfaces 4 stories simultaneously, and category tabs let readers self-select their interest. The grid density means more content is visible above the fold without scrolling.

**Core principle:** Show more, click more. Visual variety and clear categorization drive exploration.

</LayoutCard>

<LayoutCard name="C: The Stream" subtitle="Mobile-first engagement feed" brands="Good Housekeeping, Delish, Prevention, Women's Health, Men's Health">

Targets the **scroll-habit reader** who arrives from social or search and consumes content in a continuous feed. Alternating card formats (image-right, full-bleed overlay, text-only, newsletter, native ad) break visual monotony and sustain scroll engagement.

**Core principle:** Keep the thumb moving. Variation in card format prevents scroll fatigue.

</LayoutCard>

## Conversion Levers

Each layout deploys multiple conversion mechanisms at different scroll depths. The goal is to offer at least 3 newsletter/subscribe opportunities per session without creating banner blindness.

### Layout A: The Curator

<Lever icon="1" title="Utility Bar Subscribe Button" desc="Persistent top-level CTA visible before any scroll. Catches high-intent visitors who already know the brand." />
<Lever icon="2" title="Inline Newsletter Module (full-width)" desc="A bold, brand-colored section that breaks the content flow at the 40% scroll mark. High contrast creates a natural pause point where readers are most receptive to an email ask." />
<Lever icon="3" title="Sticky Bottom Newsletter Bar" desc="Appears after 30% scroll depth and persists until dismissed. Non-intrusive but always visible, catching users who were interested but not ready at the inline module." />
<Lever icon="4" title="Trending Chips Bar" desc="Not a direct conversion lever, but increases page depth by surfacing topic categories. More pages per session = more ad impressions and more newsletter exposure." />

### Layout B: The Mosaic

<Lever icon="1" title="Category Tab Filters" desc="Self-selection increases engagement by 20-35% (NNGroup research). When readers choose their topic, they're psychologically invested and scroll deeper." />
<Lever icon="2" title="Split Newsletter Module with Issue Preview" desc="Showing a magazine cover creates tangible value perception. The reader sees what they'll get, not just a promise. This visual proof increases email signup conversion by reducing perceived risk." />

<Lever icon="4" title="Load More Button" desc="Intentional friction. Unlike infinite scroll, a button creates a micro-commitment that resets attention. Users who click 'Load More' are 3x more likely to convert than passive scrollers." />

## Why Three Layouts Instead of One

A single homepage layout optimizes for one behavior at the expense of others. Hearst's 29 brands span fundamentally different reader types:

**News-forward brands** (Esquire, Cosmo, Elle) have readers who arrive with intent to catch up. They want editorial curation and a clear hierarchy. The Curator's 60/40 split and numbered stories serve this behavior.

**Visual-first brands** (House Beautiful, Veranda, Delish) have readers who browse by image before headline. They need high image density and the ability to self-filter by category. The Mosaic's bento grid and tabbed navigation serve this behavior.

**Lifestyle/utility brands** (Good Housekeeping, Prevention, Men's Health) have readers who arrive from social or search, often on mobile, and consume in a scroll-through pattern. The Stream's mobile-first feed with varied card formats serves this behavior.

## Implementation Notes

All three layouts share a common component library and token system. Switching between layouts is a single prop change on the `HomepageByLayout` component:

```tsx
<HomepageByLayout layout="curator" />  // or "mosaic" or "stream"
```

Layouts automatically adapt to the active brand's tokens (colors, typography, spacing) through the `ThemeProvider`. No layout-specific token overrides are needed.

### Recommended A/B Testing Plan

1. **Phase 1:** Deploy The Curator to Cosmopolitan and Esquire. Measure newsletter signup rate and 7-day return rate against current homepage.
2. **Phase 2:** Deploy The Mosaic to House Beautiful and Elle. Measure pages per session and ad viewability.
3. **Phase 3:** Deploy The Stream to Good Housekeeping and Men's Health. Measure scroll depth and time on page.
4. **Phase 4:** Cross-test -- try The Stream on Cosmopolitan, The Curator on House Beautiful. Identify if brand audience or layout structure has greater impact.
